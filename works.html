San Francisco has 40,000 empty homes, nearly 1 in 10 citywide.

Many of these units are kept empty for profit-driven reasons with real estate speculators using housing solely as an investment. The vast majority of empty San Francisco homes are in buildings with more than three units, and the buildings are in neighborhoods where the most market-rate housing has been built recently: Downtown, SOMA, and Mission Bay.

Many San Franciscan’s pay exorbitant amounts of money for rent and mortgages — leaving units empty only exacerbates the problem. Meanwhile, we’ve all had close friends and family pushed out of the city because of how expensive it is.

A Mission Bay apartment building sits with starkly visible vacancies

How the Empty Homes Tax Works

The Invisible Housing Crisis. Our plan is to tax vacancies in buildings with three or more units. Multi-unit buildings — especially those owned by corporate landlords — are the biggest source of the vacancy problem.

How The Empty Homes Tax Will Help: The vacancy tax is based on the square footage of a unit plus how many years it has been left vacant. The tax will get more housing units back on the market — where they rent quickly in San Francisco — and out of the pockets of corporate landlords hoarding multiple vacancies in large apartment complexes. Investment speculators are the main target of the Empty Homes Tax: the more vacant units held back from the market, the more fees.

Less vacancies = more housing. The city’s Budget and Legislative Analyst estimates that the Empty Homes Tax will immediately put 4,500 empty units back on the market. That’s thousands of units online without construction or wait time — some in rent controlled buildings.

More money for affordable housing. For the landlords who opt to pay for the tax, it is estimated that the tax will raise $38 million annually to be split between the acquisition of more affordable housing and rental subsidies for low-income families and seniors. These funds are desperately needed to build housing for working class communities of color.

Our affordability crisis makes it impossible for working people, artists and families to make it in the city we call home and love. Let’s fill empty homes by passing our vacancy tax in November!
